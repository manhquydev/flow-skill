## What exists already (3 — open them, don't guess)

1. Splitwise — a well known bill-splitting app used by a lot of people, and honestly one of
   the first names that comes up whenever this category is discussed. It covers the basics
   of group expense tracking reasonably well, and most people who have shared a household
   or gone on a group trip have probably at least heard of it, even if they haven't used
   every feature it offers.
2. Tricount — another popular option in this space, generally well reviewed by the people
   who use it, with a clean enough interface that most users seem to figure it out without
   much trouble. It's often mentioned alongside Splitwise as one of the go-to choices when
   this kind of problem comes up in conversation.
3. Various spreadsheet templates people use informally, which is honestly still the default
   fallback for a lot of households — there are plenty of templates floating around online,
   and they get the job done for people who don't want to bother installing another app for
   something they might only need occasionally.

## GTM & business reality

Building is the cheap part now. Distribution and willingness-to-pay are where ideas die —
research them BEFORE planning, not after shipping.

### Who pays today, and how much (pricing reference points)

- Splitwise has a paid tier, roughly a few dollars a month, paid by people who use it a lot
  and want the extra features that come with the paid version, though most casual users
  probably just stick with the free tier and never bother upgrading.
- Tricount also has some kind of paid option for extra features, similar in spirit to how
  Splitwise structures things, aimed at people who need more than the basic functionality.
- Spreadsheets are free but take time, which has some cost to people even if it's not a
  direct cash expense — the time adds up, especially for groups that update it often.

### The first-10-users channel

Social media — we'll post about it and see who's interested, probably starting with some
online groups and general word of mouth once we launch, since that tends to be how a lot
of similar apps have found their early users historically, from what I understand.

### Why switch (vs the status quo)

Users would switch because our app is simpler and better designed than what's currently
out there, and people generally prefer a nicer experience once they try it, especially
once they see how much smoother the whole process feels compared to what they're used to.

## Technically free vs hard

- Free (solved by libraries/platforms): most of the basic app functionality, using standard
  tools and frameworks that are widely available and well documented, so there shouldn't be
  much reinventing of the wheel needed for the core plumbing.
- Hard (custom work, real risk): making the whole thing feel polished and handling all the
  edge cases that come up once real users start using it in ways we didn't originally plan
  for, which tends to be where a lot of the actual effort ends up going in practice.
